Roles & Responsibilities

Quick reference for pilot ownership — based on the PMO AI Pilot Playbook

The Two-Person Ownership Model
Every pilot has exactly two owners — a Pilot Lead and a Pilot Partner. Co-ownership is never assigned — both people must volunteer. The role split exists to remove ambiguity, not divide accountability. Shared accountability without clear roles is how pilots quietly stall.
Role 01
Pilot Lead
Owns the momentum, all seven check-ins, and the Day 90 presentation.
Owns
  • Setting up the pilot and orienting the test group
  • Drafting and sending all written check-in updates to Level 5 (Days 1, 15, 30, 45, 75)
  • Leading verbal check-ins at Days 60 and 90
  • Keeping the pilot on schedule across all seven milestones
  • Presenting findings verbally to Level 5 at Day 90
  • Being the single point of contact Level 5 calls if there is silence
Relationship to Level 5
Primary contact. Sends all check-ins. Level 5 directs all questions and feedback to the Pilot Lead first.
The Rule
If there is silence, Level 5 reaches out to the Pilot Lead.
Role 02
Pilot Partner
Owns the validation, the challenge, and the continuity if the Lead is pulled away.
Owns
  • Independently testing the tool and tracking their own usage data
  • Providing honest, unfiltered feedback — including when the tool is not working
  • Leading the Day 45 peer review (the "third eye" step)
  • Reviewing and co-signing the Day 90 findings before presentation
  • Stepping up to full Lead responsibilities if the Lead is unavailable
Relationship to Level 5
Copied on all check-ins. Attends the Day 90 presentation. Steps in as primary contact only if the Lead is unavailable.
The Rule
If the Pilot Lead is unavailable, the Pilot Partner becomes the Lead.
Role 03
Level 5 Leadership
Receives updates, provides feedback, coaches the co-owners, and makes the final call at Day 90.
Owns
  • Receiving written check-in updates at Days 1, 15, 30, 45, and 75
  • Attending verbal check-ins at Days 60 and 90
  • Responding to written check-ins with feedback within one week
  • Making the final Standardize / Reevaluate / Table decision at Day 90
  • Approving any early pilot entry at Day 30 (exception only)
Not in the ownership seat
Level 5 does not own the pilot. They are informed, not accountable for day-to-day execution. Approval comes at Day 90 only.
The Rule
Nothing becomes a Standard without explicit Level 5 approval. No exceptions.
Why this model works for our team
Our team operates in silos and people are frequently pulled into competing delivery priorities. A single owner is vulnerable — if they are pulled away, the pilot dies quietly. Two owners prevents that. And because co-ownership is always opted into rather than assigned, both people arrive with genuine commitment — not compliance.
But two willing owners without a clear role split creates a different problem: diffusion, where both people do less because each assumes the other has the lead. The Pilot Lead / Pilot Partner model keeps the redundancy of two owners while eliminating the ambiguity. The Lead is accountable for momentum. The Partner is accountable for honest validation. Together, they produce a richer, more credible Day 90 finding than either could alone — and Level 5 gets two independent perspectives, not one advocate.

How Research Supports This Process

The Map → Visualize → Simplify → Automate sequence is grounded in decades of evidence. Click each card to learn why the order matters.

This research spans McKinsey, BCG, MIT CISR, Bain, Deloitte, Harvard Business Review, and peer-reviewed academic journals. It synthesizes three decades of evidence on what separates organizations that successfully transform from those that don't. The sequence isn't a preference — it's a pattern.
🗺️
Why Map? — Phase 1: Current State
You cannot improve what you cannot see
▼ Learn more

Here's what the research keeps coming back to: you cannot improve what you cannot see. Most teams think they know their workflows until someone asks them to draw one out together — and then the room goes quiet. That silence? That's the work.

Seventy percent of digital transformations fail — and BCG's study of 900+ transformations finds the dominant failure mode is not technological. It's automating broken processes and standardizing workflows nobody actually follows. McKinsey documents cases where automation business-case projections shrank from 80% to under 10% once development began, because underlying processes were never mapped or simplified first.

Rummler and Brache's foundational research established something we still feel today: most process failures occur in the white space between functions — at handoff points, not within teams. A telecommunications company they studied found 183 disconnects in a single customer billing process. You can't find what you haven't mapped.

For a team that is siloed and semi-standardized, mapping is not a preliminary step. It is the foundation everything else stands on. Three workflows mapped per month isn't slow — it's the pace that actually sticks.

Why Visualize? — Phase 2: Shared View
The bottleneck is alignment, not information
▼ Learn more

Something shifts when a team sees their work together for the first time. Not reads about it. Not hears about it. Sees it. That shift — from individual understanding to collective understanding — is where alignment actually begins.

Swimlane diagrams are the single best tool for making cross-functional handoffs visible. Rummler and Brache called the spaces between organizational boxes "white space" — and documented that most process failures occur there, not within any single team. Swimlanes make that white space impossible to ignore.

MIT CISR's research across 200+ companies found that organizations with a defined operating model — a shared picture of how they work — reported 31% higher operational efficiency, 33% higher customer satisfaction, and 34% faster new product development. A shared visual is not decoration. It's infrastructure.

BTS's own research puts it plainly: "The bottleneck is no longer information or coordination — it's alignment." You build alignment by creating a shared view first. Everything else follows from that.

Why Simplify? — Phase 3: Redesign
Don't pave the cow paths
▼ Learn more

Michael Hammer said it in 1990 and it remains the single most important principle in process work: "Stop paving the cow paths." Don't embed outdated processes in systems and software. Obliterate them and start over. The research since has only reinforced it.

Every major consulting firm converges on the same sequence: Eliminate → Synchronize → Streamline → Automate. McKinsey documents a bank that used this approach and reduced processing time from 12–15 days to 6–8 days, automating 70% of steps. The sequence isn't optional — it's the mechanism. Organizations that reverse this order fail at rates exceeding 70%.

Münstermann, Eckhardt, and Weitzel's empirical study of 156 firms found that business process standardization explains nearly 62% of the variance in process performance (R² = 0.619). The mechanism: common definitions make processes transparent, enable benchmarking through shared KPIs, and reduce ambiguity in cross-boundary communication.

Bain found that 85% of CEOs in companies that ceased to be value creators blame internal complexity — much of it stemming from process fragmentation. Simplification isn't housekeeping. It's strategy.

Why Automate Last? — Phase 4: Automate
Automation on a broken process is a faster broken process
▼ Learn more

Automation is powerful. It's also the most expensive way to make a broken process run faster. The research on this is unambiguous — and a little uncomfortable.

MIT CISR's Jeanne Ross puts it plainly: "If you don't have a strong operational backbone, the platform you build is much more likely to be a waste of money." BCG found that 93% of 2,600 executives surveyed struggled to scale digital solutions because underlying process foundations were missing.

McKinsey (2025) warns of a new version of this same problem in the AI era: "agent sprawl" — the uncontrolled proliferation of redundant, fragmented, and ungoverned AI agents across teams and functions. It mirrors early RPA sprawl, where individual tools multiplied without governance and compounded fragmentation rather than reducing it.

HBR/Deloitte found that mature process definitions produce 19% back-office capacity gains versus just 12% for organizations without mature standards — a 58% relative improvement in automation returns. The sequence is the ROI.

Automate last — not because automation isn't powerful, but because automation applied to a simplified, standardized process is the only kind that actually scales.
